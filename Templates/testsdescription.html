Tests



T-Tests

Determines if there is a significant difference between the means of two groups, commonly used when comparing means of small sample sizes or when the population standard deviation is unknown.

Null and Alternative Hypothesis

Null hypothesis posits no significant difference, while the alternative hypothesis suggests otherwise, and statistical tests aim to determine whether there is enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis.

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

Assesses whether the means of three or more groups are significantly different from each other by comparing the variability within groups to the variability between groups.

Chi-Square Test

Determines if there is a significant association between two categorical variables by comparing observed frequencies to expected frequencies.

Mann-Whitney U Test

Non-parametric test used to determine if there is a significant difference between the distributions of two independent groups, particularly when the assumptions of the t-test are violated.

K-S Test (Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test)

Assesses if two samples come from the same distribution by comparing their cumulative distribution functions.

Shapiro-Wilk Test

Determines whether a sample comes from a normally distributed population by comparing observed data to the expected normal distribution.

Durbin-Watson Test

Assesses whether there is autocorrelation present in the residuals of a regression model, particularly used for time series data.

Breusch-Godfrey Test

Tests for autocorrelation in a regression model's residuals up to a specified lag order, extending the Durbin-Watson test to higher orders.

Heteroscedasticity Test

Evaluates whether the variance of the errors in a regression model is constant across observations, which is crucial for the validity of regression analyses.

Multicollinearity Test

Assesses the degree of collinearity among independent variables in a regression model, which can affect the model's stability and interpretation.
